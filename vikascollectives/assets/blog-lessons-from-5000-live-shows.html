Behind the Music

What 5,000 Live Shows Taught Me About Audiences

Three decades on stages across India and the world — and the lessons that only live audiences can teach.

Somewhere between an embassy evening in Delhi and a corporate gala abroad, I crossed 5,000 live shows. Across India, Australia, the UK, Hong Kong, Dubai, Nepal, Canada and many more countries, audiences taught me lessons no music school ever could.

Audiences everywhere want the same thing

The venues change — a palace lawn in Rajasthan, a harbour-view hall in Hong Kong, a winter ballroom in Canada — but the audience's wish never does. People want to feel something familiar, delivered with genuine skill. A Bollywood melody in Dubai, a jazz standard in Delhi: recognition plus craft is the universal formula.

The first ninety seconds decide the night

Audiences make up their minds about a performer almost immediately. I choose openers the way a chef chooses a first course — something warm, familiar and confident. Win the first song and the room gives you permission to take them anywhere for the next two hours.

Reading the room beats any set list

I plan every show, and I abandon the plan at almost every show. When a corporate crowd in London wanted Bollywood, they got Bollywood. When a Delhi sangeet crowd wanted jazz with their cocktails, jazz it was. The set list serves the room, never the other way around.

Silence is part of the music

The most powerful moments in 5,000 shows were often the quietest — a held note as a bride entered, a pause before a final phrase. Amateurs fill every second; experience teaches you to leave space.

Show 5,001

Every performance still gets the same preparation as the first one did thirty years ago, because for the people in that room, it is not my 5,000th show — it is their one wedding, their one annual gala, their one evening. That is the real lesson: there are no routine shows, only unrepeatable nights.
